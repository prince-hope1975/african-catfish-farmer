## 1.1 INTRODUCTION OF AQUACULTURE IN NIGERIA

Aquaculture is the production of aquatic organisms such as shrimps, fish, shellfish

and seaweeds. In Nigeria however, aquaculture is almost limited to fish farming only.

The species which are suitable to farm in Nigeria, are limited too. Depending on the biology of the species, market, availability of seed and suitable feeds, I consider three species to be feasible for farming in Nigeria: African catfish (Clarias gariepinus), Tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) and Carp (Cyprinus carpio). From these three species the vast

majority of the production is of African catfish also called the walking catfish or barbel,

the local name in South Africa. In this handbook I only focus on the production of African catfish, because it has a wide acceptance in Nigeria, it grows very quickly and it can be economically farmed in small scale enterprises with relatively low investments. On the African continent fish farming is not widespread. There are only two countries

which really have developed fish farming: Egypt and Nigeria. Egypt is mainly focusing

on tilapia production and Nigeria is mainly focusing on African catfish production.

In the early days after the Nigerian independence, 'fish farming' and 'fishing' were mixed up terms, but they have a totally different meaning. Fish farming is done in a controlled water body, fish are stocked and fed deliberately. After a growing period,

the fish are harvested in a controlled manner to easily crop the fish. Fishing is catching

fish in natural water bodies with seine nets, fish traps, angling and other fishing equipment. What is caught is always a surprise. The term 'fisheries' is actually covering both activities.

In the early seventies the first pioneers started with real fish farming, driven by a

growing population and increased prices for fish. They however faced a serious problem: the know-how for fish farming was not available and good quality feeds and seeds were very scarce.

In the eighties fish farming started to change through the introduction of new farming

techniques, which resulted in increased fingerling production of catfish, and the

introduction of imported starter feed.

In the nineties fish farming really started to boom and the RAS (recirculation aqua culture system) was introduced. This system is highly intensive and does not need

much space. You actually can farm successfully in the middle of town. The awareness

of fish farming increased exponentially and already a thousand fish farmers had started their own farm. However, in the rush of starting, many farmers stopped their farming activities as fast as they had started them and lost a lot of money. A cold-blooded animal or ectotherm, like all fish are, does not have an internal mechanism

to regulate body temperature. Instead, it relies on solar energy captured by the

environment. Catfish will always have a body temperature which is equal to the ambient water temperature. The internal activity of the fish, called its metabolism, increases with increasing water temperature, but it has an optimal range. For the African sharptooth catfish the optimal body temperature varies between 25 and $ 3 0^{\circ} \mathrm{C} $

So, cold-blooded means that a fish is not internally generating energy to regulate its

body temperature. Therefore, it requires far less energy than warmblooded animals or endotherms. Warm-blooded animals need frequent meals on a daily basis to keep the body temperature constant, whereas coldblooded animals can skip meals for days and even for weeks without any negative health impact.

At our farm, we always take into consideration the temperature of the water and the activity of the fish.

## 3.1 INTRODUCTION

Catfish farming has to be carried out in fresh water. The salt content (sodium chloride) should

not exceed 6 g/l. Unfortunately, most of the water available in the world is salt water. The

proportions are as follows: 97.5% of the water reserves in the world consists of salt water (oceans), 1.75% in the form of ice and snow on glaciers at the North and South Poles, 0.5% is underground water and only 0.25% is fresh water from rivers, swamps and lakes.

Africa is the continent with the lowest availability of fresh water; actually many places in

Africa have to deal with a shortage of water. This makes fish farming economically unfeasible.

In Nigeria however, we have places with abundant water from rivers, streams and dams, especially in the south and west. In the north we face shortages of water and rivers dry up during the dry season. Underground water is available almost everywhere in Nigeria, but the yield of boreholes and the water quality vary a lot from area to area.

Let us evaluate each source of water.

## 3.2 WATER FROM A RIVER OR STREAM

Rivers and streams in Nigeria which have water flowing all year round, are very suitable

sources of water for fish farming. To find out if the water quality is suitable for fish farming, the first thing to discover is whether the river has an active fishing community. Get your information concerning the fish population and catches from the locals, find out the lowest and highest water level in the river and whether there are any activities which have a negative

impact on the water quality and quantity.

Unfortunately, rivers and streams in Nigeria are also used for waste disposal, solid as well as liquid. Another activity which has a really bad impact on the river, is mining. Stones are crushed on the riverside and the river water is used for washing. Chemicals are used to

isolate the precious minerals from the sand. As a result, the water becomes extremely turbid

and when the flow of water in the river decreases during the dry season, it becomes a poor source of water for fish farming.

Another problem with river water arises with the first rains. When they start, the river water gets polluted with runoff from farmland. The water not only becomes turbid but also gets

polluted with insecticides and herbicides. The effect is an increasing rate of catfish mortality,

because this pollution weakens their immune system. As a result, bacterial infections are prone to happen. During the peak of the rainy season, the runoff is less poisonous to the fish and consequently the mortality rate reduces. The best time of the year for fish farming in for instance the Abuja region is the peak of the dry season, because the water is clear and less

polluted, but this can be different for other regions in Nigeria.

If the relationship between the size of the pond and the number of fish is not relevant, how do I calculate the number of fish I can stock in my farm?

The stocking density in a pond is related to the flow of water which passes through the pond. In other words, the more water you are able to flush out of the pond, the more fish

The feed is consumed by the growing fish, but this process also causes pollution in the form of faeces, urine, ammonia and carbon dioxide. All these accumulating waste products will

cause serious toxic problems for the fish. Therefore they have to be removed. This is only

possible by flushing the water periodically or constantly out of the pond.

The relationship between the feed and the flushing rate is as follows: for every 1 kg of feed given to the fish, you need to flush 1.4 m $ ^{3} $ of water.

Sedimentation tank

Sedimentation is a relatively easy process. The flow rate in the sedimentation tank (Photo 7) is slow and allows the particles which are heavier than water, to settle before flowing out. As

a result, the particles will settle onto the bottom. When the tank includes sedimentation filter

materials, it increases the settling rate and trapping of solid particles, which results in overflow water without any particles from the suspended solids. The next step is to regularly flush and rinse the sedimentation tank, otherwise the solids start to dissolve and have a negative impact on the nitrification process. By flushing the sedimentation tank, you also refresh the water. As a result,

the salt levels will not keep on increasing but stay in balance. A good sedimentation tank can

reduce the load of fish waste by 50%. A sedimentation tank can also include bacterial processes such as denitrification. This occurs when oxygen levels in the tank are close to zero and the organic load (faeces) is eaten by bacteria which in their turn produce nitrogen gas out of nitrate.

Degassing unit in order to remove carbon dioxide

When the fish metabolise, they produce carbon dioxide. This gas will partly be dissolved in the water which causes a problem at high concentrations. Carbon dioxide tends to make the

fish sluggish which reduces their ability to eat. Compare it to a poorly ventilated room which

you are in. The only thing you want to do is sleep. The carbon dioxide is easily removed with a properly functioning biofiltration unit. In the biofiltration unit oxygen will diffuse into the water and carbon dioxide will diffuse into the air. When the water has passed this unit, it is in a good condition for the fish and will go straight to the fish tank.

No special unit is needed, because a properly functioning biofiltration unit will automatically

take care of degassing the water.

Starting up a recirculation system

A brand-new recirculation system has no bacteria inside the filter. The good thing is that bacteria will move in spontaneously the moment you run the system and fish

release their waste products. These are food for the bacteria. It takes about six weeks to

develop a fully-grown bacterial mass on the filter media. During this period, the levels of ammonium become high in the first two weeks. Thereafter high levels of nitrite levels arise for about three weeks. In this period, you have to flush a lot of water in order not to poison your fish. Also start with small fish or fry so that the load of waste is small and

the bacterial mass can grow alongside the fish.

You can also buy nitrifying bacteria in a bottle and add them to the system. This start-up will be faster. Another method is to start up the filter without fish by just adding a little feed or urea into the system and allowing it to run for some weeks. The third method consists of putting a spare filter media in a tank submerged in the water aerated and fed

with some fish feed or urea. The filter media, when covered with bacteria, are slimy and

can be used in a system to speed up the nitrifying process.

Medication and recirculation systems

In a recirculation system you can medicate the fish, but depending on the medicine or

chemicals you use, this can affect the performance of the biofilter.

Treatment with chemicals, such as formalin and potassium permanganate, will kill some of the bioflora on the filter media. After treatment, extra flushing is required to keep the levels of ammonium and nitrite low. Normally the bacterial mass will return within

a week.

Other medicines, such as antibiotics, usually have no effect at all on the bioflora of the media. They specifically kill the pathogens and not the bacteria in the filter.

Salt, widely used to suppress bacterial growth and parasites, has no effect on the bioflora

of the media.

When the fish are given medicines via a bath treatment (see chapter 9 on fish diseases), it's advisable to treat the complete system and not only the culture tank. Apply the chemical in

the fish tank, but keep the pump running. By doing it this way, you avoid the fish getting

stressed. If a chemical like formalin is used, you have to increase the flushing rate in order

to remove the formalin after some hours.

The effectiveness of the medicines usually reduces with time, because they are not stable

in a water solution and the filter breaks them down too. Especially oxytetracycline which

breaks down rapidly. The water will turn dark brown within twelve hours after applying it.

Don't bother to flush it out, since the catfish like this dark tea coloured water.

Maintenance of a recirculation system

A recirculation system requires maintenance. The major maintenance consists of keeping

the components clean. When the pump gets dirty, the yield of the pump reduces gradually. It's important to clean the impeller on a monthly basis or every time the system is emptied after a growth cycle. If the system uses UV-light, the quartz glass should also be cleaned after every cycle, and checked if the UV-light still works. Ultraviolet radiation kills pathogens

and keeps the water transparent, but if the quartz glass of the UV-light is dirty, the radiation

is not optimal and will not do anything. Also, the UV-light reduces in efficiency and should be replaced on a yearly basis, even if it's still radiating UV-rays. Ultraviolet light can harm the eyes, if the eyes are directly exposed to it. So, be very careful not to look at the UV-light directly. After every growth cycle, the pipes of the recirculation system should be cleaned

inside and outside. Therefore, the pipes should have some fittings which can be easily

disconnected. Some parts inside the tank shouldn't be glued at all. Pipe brushes with a long pole can be helpful.

Any time you empty the system, the cleaning should start immediately because if the dirt dries up on the surfaces of the tank and other components, it becomes very hard to get

rid of. The filter media should be cleaned as well. A high pressure cleaner works very well

for this purpose. After cleaning, you can start another growth cycle by putting a new set of fish in the tank. If you want to do another spawning in the cleaned system, it's also advisable to disinfect with chlorine or hydrogen peroxide. After thoroughly rinsing, the system is ready for another spawning. Because the filter media will not have any bacteria

yet, keep in mind that the recirculation system has to start up afresh.

The disinfection procedure is discussed in chapter 8 on reproduction because in hatcheries disinfection is very important in order to get continuously good results.

## Predators

Putting cages in a natural environment, attracts a lot of fish-eating birds and if not taken care of, this will certainly lead to severe losses. The most common birds are the kingfisher, the heron, the hammerhead bird and the cormorant. Within days of stocking, they will spot your site and immediately start to hunt; even when you have not seen any of those

birds when building the project. The evidence apart from seeing them on the cages, are

the faeces, feathers and wounded fish. A bird net to protect your fish on top of the cage is obligatory.

Less obvious are the predators in the water, for instance monitor lizards, when cages are close to shore, crocodiles and tiger fish. If they enter the cages, they have already damaged

the nets. The loss mainly consists of fish that have escaped through the gaps in the nets,

because more fish escape than the predators actually eat. To overcome this problem, you can put an extra strong net around the net in which the fish are contained. This net is called a predator net, which is a very strong coarse 3 to 5 cm diameter netting. The net cannot be damaged by crocodiles or any other animal with sharp teeth. An extra advantage is that

fish which escape from the first net, will still be trapped in the second net as long as they

are big enough. Between the first net and the predator net a population of young wild fish can grow and eventually not escape the predator net anymore because of their bigger size by then. 5. 3 PLASTIC LINED POND

If you are interested in building a natural pond but the soil does not hold water, you

can dig a pond in the same shape as an earthen pond and put a plastic lining in (Photo 15). For a plastic lined pond, it is important that the surface of the soil is smooth and does not contain any sharp objects which can puncture the lining. Also, the lining should be 1 mm thick and UV-resistant. UV-resistant means that sunlight doesn't spoil

the lining and eventually cause it to crack after some time. The standard UV-resistant

lining is not available in Nigeria, so it has to be imported. If the pond is big, the lining will be delivered in pieces and will have to be welded together. This is a job for a specialist with a special welding machine. If the welding is not carried out well, after

some months or years the lining will just fall apart due to the detrimental effects of the

wind and other weather conditions.

## 5.9 POND SIZE AND NUMBER

When you are ready to start building ponds, you probably have already chosen the shape of the pond and the material you want to use, but you have not yet decided how big the

ponds should be. How big should a pond be? For the on-growing stage of the growth

cycle, the pond should be of a size which enables the stocked fish to be sold within a week when harvest is due. You also have to determine the number

of sales days you want to have in a year. The more times you want to sell the more ponds you need to build. It is advisable to have regular sales, for example on a weekly basis. This allows you to build a

customer base with regular customers.

Example 6. How to calculate the number of ponds you need?

Suppose your production is 100 tons a year and you want to sell on a weekly basis.

This means 52 sales moments or days in a year. Let's assume the growth cycle of the

fish is 175 days; in this case the fish remain in the pond from stocking till harvest

during 175 days. This means you can do 2 cycles in a year. Each pond has 2 harvests in a year. So, for sales on a weekly basis, you need 52/2=26 ponds! This means you stock on a weekly basis and you harvest on a weekly basis. If you want to sell on a monthly basis, you need 12/2=6 ponds.
